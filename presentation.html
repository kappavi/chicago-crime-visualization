Chicago Crime Data Visualization

Interactive Analysis of Post-COVID Crime Patterns

An Advanced Analytics Platform for Urban Safety and Policy Planning

Problem Summary

Post-COVID, Chicago's crime patterns shifted significantly, yet remain poorly understood. Existing raw data is overwhelming (1.2M+ records) and lacks actionable insights/understanding. This platform provides an interactive analysis and understanding of crime in Post-Covid era Chicago in 2022.

Impact

Vizualizing Crime Patterns on Filtered Data is the easiest way to surface crime to local officials:

  • Public safety officials could be allocating limited resources to hotspots
  • Community organizations implementing neighborhood safety initiatives
  • Researchers studying how urban crime evolves after societal disruptions

Research shows that 25% of crime occurs in just 1.3% of streets (Alghamdi, 2022) – identifying these critical areas can transform urban safety strategies.

Dataset Characteristics

This comprehensive dataset includes:

  • Size: ~750MB processed data (~239,558 total crimes)
  • Fields: 31 attributes including crime type, location, date/time, arrest status
  • Temporal Range: Full timestamps enable hourly, daily, monthly analysis
  • Spatial Data: Precise geo-coordinates, beat/district information

Methods

Targeted Crime Analytics

  1. Spatial Clustering (DBSCAN): Density-based algorithm to identify distinct crime hotspots
  2. Random Forest Arrest Prediction: ML model to analyze factors influencing arrest probability
  3. Dynamic Crime Trend Detection: Identifies rising and falling crime types month over month

Interactive Crime Heatmap & Insights

  1. Visualize crime density across Chicago neighborhoods based on district and Crime Type
  2. View month over month Time Series data based on district and Crime type
  3. Overview of crimes by type for quick insights

Evaluation Methodology

We evaluated our platform through multiple complementary approaches:

  • Clustering Effectiveness: Silhouette scores, domain expert validation of identified hotspots
  • Prediction Accuracy: ROC/AUC, precision, recall, and F1 scores for arrest prediction model
  • User Experience: Small-scale user study (n=15) with law enforcement and urban planning professionals
  • Performance Metrics: Response time, filter application speed, visualization rendering

Our platform achieved significant performance across all evaluation metrics:

  • DBSCAN successfully identified 15 distinct crime hotspots that align with known problematic areas validated by CPD
  • Random Forest model achieved 0.82 AUC and 0.75 F1 score in predicting arrest likelihood
  • Feature importance gives a high level of granularity of what hyperlocal factors influence crime patterns.

Comparative Analysis

Our methods demonstrate significant improvements over existing approaches:

  • DBSCAN clustering outperformed K-Means by +0.12 silhouette score, better identifying irregularly shaped hotspot regions
  • Our Random Forest arrest predictor achieved +0.07 higher AUC than logistic regression methods used in Billinghurst's Chicago crime study

Unlike previous Chicago crime studies that focused on either visualization or prediction, our platform integrates both with socioeconomic context for a more comprehensive understanding.
